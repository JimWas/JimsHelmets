[ SECTION 1: THE CATASTROPHIC CRISIS ]

Visual Element: A split graphic comparing household economics. On the left, a visualization of an average rural household pile of money. On the right, a giant red medical bill overshadowing it.

Data Callouts:

Average Rural Annual Income: $\approx \$2,000\text{ USD}$ per year (just $\$166\text{ USD}$ per month).

Acute Neuro-Trauma Care Cost: $\$1,500\text{ USD}$ to $\$2,500\text{ USD}$ (representing $75\%$ to $125\%$ of the family's entire annual GDP contribution).

Technical Callout (LaTeX Formula):


$$\text{Immediate Financial Shock} \geq 100\% \text{ of Gross Annual Income}$$

[ SECTION 2: THE ANATOMY OF SYSTEMIC RUIN (THE STEP-FLOW) ]

Visual Direction: A vertical timeline or pathway using down-pointing arrow connectors to guide the donor through the financial chain reaction.

Step 1: The Crash

Icon: Motorcycle silhouette with an impact point.

Copy: A family traveling to a local school is clipped. The child, riding unprotected, suffers a severe Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI). Emergency neurosurgery is the only way to save their life.

Step 2: The Credit Trap

Icon: Hand shaking under a shadow / Money lender.

Copy: Lacking universal insurance and formal bank collateral, the desperate family cannot access traditional credit under the National Bank's strict interest caps. They turn to local informal lenders ("Kruor").

Data Callout: Interest rates compound at $2.5\%$ to $5\%$ per month—an effective $35\% \text{ to } 79.5\% \text{ APR}$.

Step 3: Asset Stripping & Land Loss

Icon: A farm tractor crossed out / A document with a red stamp.

Copy: To secure the emergency loan, the family is forced to hand over their agricultural "soft title" (land rights) as collateral. As interest compound-multipliers outpace their farming yields, they must sell their land to settle the debt.

The Impact: The family loses their primary means of production. They transition instantly from independent farm owners to landless, low-wage manual day laborers.

Step 4: Sibling Sacrifice (The Sibling Substitution)

Icon: A school backpack left on the ground next to brick-making tools.

Copy: Debt service eats up $100\%$ of remaining family cash flow. Direct schooling costs (uniforms, transportation, books) are cut. The uninjured older sibling is pulled out of school to enter the labor force (brick kilns or garment factories) to help pay off the hospital loan.

Data Callout: School dropout rates rise by $30\%$ among rural Cambodian families following a household road injury.

[ SECTION 3: THE PARALLEL FUTURES (SIDE-BY-SIDE COMPARE) ]

Visual Direction: Two distinct vertical paths showing the trajectory of two identical families over a 15-year horizon.

Path A: Without Jim's Helmet (The Intergenerational Trap)

Visual: A dark, descending staircase icon.

Year 1: Severe crash $\rightarrow$ Private moneylender loan taken at $50\% \text{ APR}$.

Year 2: Household land sold to pay medical bill $\rightarrow$ Sibling drops out of Grade 7.

Year 5: Sibling enters low-wage garment manufacturing to support the disabled child.

Year 15: Sibling's own children start life landless, lacking education. Cycle repeats.

Path B: With Jim's Helmet (The Safety Dividend)

Visual: A bright, upward-trending arrow icon.

Year 1: Identical crash $\rightarrow$ Certified EPS liner deforms, slowing brain deceleration to safety. Child suffers mild concussion, discharged within 48 hours.

Year 2: Total bill is less than $\$100\text{ USD}$ $\rightarrow$ Paid from normal household savings. No moneylenders involved.

Year 5: Family keeps their land. Both children remain in school.

Year 15: Sibling graduates high school, entering the urban services economy at a higher wage tier. The cycle is broken.
