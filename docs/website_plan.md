# Jim's Helmets Website Plan

## Goal

Build a state-of-the-art, sleek, easy-to-use website that turns the existing Jim's Helmets research into a credible fundraising and partnership engine for a nonprofit giving safety-certified helmets to children in Cambodia.

The site should make one idea immediately clear:

> Cambodia's child helmet crisis is preventable. Jim's Helmets combines certified tropical helmets, parent education, and school-based behavior change so every child can ride protected.

## Research Inputs Used

This plan consolidates the strongest material from:

- `README.md`: mission, problem, tropical helmet solution, school-based intervention model.
- `docs/notes.md`: core barriers: heat, lack of education, short-trip risk perception, parent fears, and the need for re-education.
- `docs/children_research.md`: child-specific helmet crisis, myths, physiology, growth economics, trade-in idea, certified helmet advocacy.
- `docs/nonprofit_plan.md`: epidemiology, law and enforcement gaps, Protec sourcing, school pathway, governance, pilot budget, impact model.
- `docs/jim_s_helmets_interactive_report.html`: problem-solution-impact narrative, impact simulator, roadmap, budget transparency.
- `docs/jim_s_helmets_feasibility_spa_infographic.html`: feasibility framing, school/hospital/police distribution model, launch milestones.
- `docs/engineering_safety_the_tropical_helmet_solution.html`: EPS protection, heat ventilation, weight myth debunking.
- `docs/executive_briefing_dashboard.html`: donor-grade executive framing, budget integrity, safety dividend.
- `docs/national_impact_investment.html`: national economic case and human-capital ROI.
- `website/`: current static starter with core pages: Home, Crisis, Solution, Work, Impact, Donate.
- `docs/Media/` and `docs/Crash_Videos/`: existing visual assets that can support a more human, less abstract site.

Before public launch, the final site should include a citation review pass for medical, legal, financial, and Cambodia-specific claims.

## Strategic Positioning

### Brand Promise

Small helmet. Big future.

### One-Sentence Mission

Jim's Helmets protects Cambodian children from preventable brain injury by pairing safety-certified tropical helmets with parent education and school-based habit-building.

### Core Message Pillars

1. The crisis is urgent.
   Road traffic injuries are a leading cause of death and disability for Cambodian children and adolescents, and child helmet use remains dangerously low.

2. The current barrier is not parental indifference.
   Parents face real barriers: heat, cost, misinformation, short-trip risk bias, inconsistent enforcement, and uncertainty about child helmet fit and safety.

3. The product must fit the climate and the child.
   The helmet story should emphasize certified EPS impact protection, low weight, ventilation, and correct sizing.

4. Helmets alone are not enough.
   The nonprofit's strongest differentiator is the 12-week school-based model: school audits, parent covenants, teacher training, handover ceremonies, curriculum reinforcement, and compliance tracking.

5. Donors fund measurable protection.
   A donor should understand what a gift buys, how it changes behavior, and how Jim's Helmets reports outcomes.

## Primary Audiences

### Individual Donors

They need an emotional, fast path to trust. The site should show the child's reality, the simple cost of protection, and a clear donation action.

### Founding Partners and Corporate Sponsors

They need seriousness: governance, budget transparency, sourcing logic, school operations, impact tracking, and partner visibility.

### Cambodian Schools and Local Partners

They need clarity on how the program works, what is expected of schools and parents, and why the program supports rather than blames families.

### Foundations and Institutional Donors

They need downloadable briefs, citations, a theory of change, measurement plan, pilot budget, governance model, and evidence from prior school-based helmet interventions.

## Recommended Site Architecture

The current six-page structure is a good starting point, but the new site should feel like one polished product. Use a fast homepage narrative with deeper secondary pages.

### Top Navigation

- The Crisis
- The Helmet
- The Program
- Impact
- Partners
- Donate

Use a sticky header with a persistent Donate button. On mobile, keep the Donate action visible in the menu and near the bottom of key pages.

### Homepage

Purpose: move visitors from urgency to belief to action in under 90 seconds.

Sections:

1. Hero
   - Full-bleed Cambodia or school commute visual.
   - H1: "Small Helmet. Big Future."
   - Supporting copy: "Jim's Helmets protects Cambodian children from preventable brain injury with certified tropical helmets and school-based safety education."
   - CTAs: "Give a Helmet" and "See the Program".

2. Three-number proof strip
   - Child helmet use gap.
   - Pilot goal: 1,000 helmets.
   - Modeled impact: deaths and severe head injuries prevented.
   - Use conservative wording such as "modeled", "estimated", or "pilot target" where applicable.

3. The real barrier
   - Heat.
   - Cost.
   - Short-trip myth.
   - Parent fear about neck and brain growth.
   - Message: "This is an education and access problem, not a blame problem."

4. The Jim's Helmets model
   - Certified tropical helmets.
   - Parent workshops.
   - School-gate habit building.
   - Trade-in sizing program.

5. Interactive impact calculator
   - Slider: 100 to 10,000 helmets.
   - Outputs: helmets funded, children protected, estimated severe injuries prevented, estimated economic value.
   - Include a small methodology link.

6. Donation band
   - "$15 can fund one direct-sourced helmet target."
   - "$25 supports helmet plus education materials."
   - "$250 protects a classroom group."
   - "$1,000 helps launch a school distribution day."

7. Trust footer
   - Governance status.
   - Contact.
   - Downloadable research brief.
   - Newsletter/signup.

### The Crisis

Purpose: explain why child passengers are uniquely vulnerable.

Content:

- Road traffic injuries as a major child and youth safety issue in Cambodia.
- Motorcycle-centered family transport.
- Child passenger helmet use gap.
- The short-trip fallacy.
- Why low-speed head impacts can be devastating for children.
- Why emergency response delays make prevention more important.

UX:

- Use a calm but urgent tone.
- Include simple diagrams, not walls of text.
- Use short "myth vs reality" panels:
  - "Short trip" vs "most risk starts close to home."
  - "Helmet is too heavy" vs "certified child helmets are lightweight."
  - "$2 plastic cap" vs "certified EPS impact protection."

### The Helmet

Purpose: build confidence that the helmet is medically and climatically appropriate.

Content:

- Certified protection: ECE 22.05/22.06 or current selected certification.
- EPS crush liner explained simply.
- Tropical ventilation: intake vents, internal ducts, rear exhaust.
- Lightweight target: around 280g for small sizes, subject to final supplier confirmation.
- Fit and sizing.
- Why uncertified "fashion helmets" are not enough.

UX:

- Use an interactive helmet explainer with hotspots:
  - Shell.
  - EPS liner.
  - Vent channel.
  - Chin strap.
  - Size label.
- Add a "Parent Questions" accordion:
  - Will it hurt my child's neck?
  - Will it affect head growth?
  - Will it make my child too hot?
  - How do I know it fits?

### The Program

Purpose: show that Jim's Helmets is a behavior-change nonprofit, not just a giveaway.

Recommended framing:

> We do not hand out helmets and hope. We build the habit around the helmet.

Content:

1. School selection
   - High-risk roads.
   - High motorcycle commute share.
   - Public primary school priority.

2. Baseline audit
   - Commute pattern survey.
   - Helmet use observation.
   - Student sizing.

3. Parent-teacher safety covenant
   - Parent workshop.
   - Myth debunking.
   - Every ride pledge.

4. Distribution ceremony
   - School leaders.
   - Traffic police.
   - Local authorities.
   - Public social proof.

5. Curriculum and safety ambassadors
   - "No Helmet, No Ride."
   - Peer reinforcement.
   - Teacher follow-up.

6. Measurement
   - School-gate checks.
   - Follow-up at 2 weeks, 12 weeks, and 3-4 months.
   - Donor reporting.

7. Sizing trade-in
   - Children grow, protection should continue.

UX:

- Show this as a horizontal timeline on desktop and stacked sequence on mobile.
- Include a downloadable "School Partner Overview" PDF later.

### Impact

Purpose: make the nonprofit feel measurable and transparent.

Content:

- Pilot target: 1,000 helmets.
- Year 1 budget model:
  - 60% helmet inventory.
  - 15% school logistics.
  - 15% parent education and materials.
  - 10% admin, legal, and compliance.
- Modeled impact:
  - 100 helmets.
  - 500 helmets.
  - 1,000 helmets.
  - 5,000 helmets.
  - 10,000 helmets.
- Outcome dashboard:
  - Helmets distributed.
  - Schools reached.
  - Parent workshops completed.
  - Follow-up helmet-use rate.
  - Trade-ins completed.

UX:

- Interactive calculator from prior prototypes.
- Budget donut or stacked bar.
- Public reporting section that can be updated monthly or quarterly.

### Partners

Purpose: create a credible path for bigger supporters.

Content:

- Schools.
- Local authorities.
- Helmet suppliers such as Protec, once confirmed.
- Corporate sponsors.
- Foundations.
- Hospitals or trauma-care partners.
- Traffic police engagement.

Partner CTA options:

- Fund a school.
- Sponsor a province.
- Donate logistics.
- Support education materials.
- Help with measurement.

UX:

- Partner cards with clear benefits and expected contribution.
- "Founding Partner" inquiry form.

### Donate

Purpose: maximize completion with minimum confusion.

Donation flow:

- Preset amounts:
  - $15: one helmet target cost through direct sourcing.
  - $25: helmet plus education support.
  - $75: protect three children.
  - $250: classroom group support.
  - $1,000: school distribution day support.
  - Custom.
- Monthly toggle.
- Payment processor: Stripe or Donorbox.
- Receipt and thank-you email.
- Optional dedication.

Trust elements:

- What your gift funds.
- Current nonprofit registration/tax status.
- Refund/contact policy.
- Privacy policy.
- Annual report link when available.

Avoid:

- A vague "Secure Donation Portal" placeholder.
- Any unsupported tax-deductibility claim before 501(c)(3) status is complete.

## Visual Direction

The site should feel modern, direct, and optimistic. Avoid charity cliches, guilt-heavy imagery, and generic stock-photo softness.

### Design Principles

- Sleek, high-contrast, fast-loading.
- Human photos and field media first.
- Dense information where donors need it, but never overwhelming.
- Donation actions visible and simple.
- Mobile-first, because many visitors will open links from social platforms.

### Palette

Recommended palette:

- Deep charcoal: trust and readability.
- Safety orange: primary call-to-action and helmet visibility.
- Tropical cyan/blue: ventilation, air, and climate engineering.
- Leaf green: Cambodia, schools, and forward motion.
- Warm off-white: background, but avoid a beige-heavy look.

### Typography

- Use a clean sans-serif such as Inter, Geist, or similar.
- Use one optional display face only for major campaign lines.
- Keep body text highly readable.

### Imagery

Use existing media where appropriate, but curate carefully:

- School commute visuals.
- Helmet fitting moments.
- Parent education moments.
- Road context in Cambodia.
- Avoid relying on crash footage in the main experience; use it only if ethically necessary and with warning/framing.

## Recommended Technical Approach

The current site is plain HTML/CSS/JS. That is fine for a prototype, but the target experience will benefit from a modern app stack.

### Recommended Stack

- Framework: Next.js.
- Hosting: Vercel.
- Styling: Tailwind CSS or a small custom design system.
- CMS: Sanity, Contentful, or a simple file-based content layer for launch.
- Donations: Stripe Checkout, Donorbox, or Givebutter depending on nonprofit status and fees.
- Database: Supabase for impact counts, partner inquiries, and newsletter signups.
- Email: Resend for transactional and thank-you emails.
- Analytics: Vercel Web Analytics or Plausible.

### Why This Stack

- Fast pages.
- Easy future expansion.
- Good SEO.
- Safe donation handoff.
- Simple content updates.
- Strong deployment workflow.

## Content Tone

Use language that respects Cambodian parents. The site should never imply parents do not care. The research points to a better message:

> Parents want their children safe. Jim's Helmets removes the barriers that make safety hard: cost, heat, misinformation, and lack of habit.

Avoid:

- "Parents are negligent."
- "We save them from themselves."
- Overstated certainty in modeled numbers.
- Legal or tax claims before verification.

Prefer:

- "Preventable."
- "Evidence-informed."
- "School-based."
- "Certified."
- "Tropical."
- "Every ride."
- "Measured over time."

## Build Phases

- Produce final sitemap and page copy.

### Phase 2: Design Prototype

- Create high-fidelity homepage.

- Design Crisis, Helmet, Program, Impact, Partners, Donate pages.

### Phase 3: Build

- Move from static starter to Next.js or upgrade static site if speed matters more than future CMS.
- Build reusable sections and components.
- Add donation integration.
- Add forms and analytics.
- Add downloadable research brief.
- Add SEO metadata and social preview images.

### Phase 4: Verification

- Test mobile and desktop.
- Test donation flow in sandbox mode.
- Test forms.
- Check accessibility.

- Connect domain.
- Enable analytics.
- Announce founding campaign.
- Begin quarterly impact reporting.
